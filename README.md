Framework 1.4 is the fifth volume in the Framework series and the first to add an immunological dimension to the series’ existing energetic/structural architecture (Framework 1.0–1.3). Its organizing question is: who sees the clone, and on what basis — that is, by what rules a cell is recognized by the immune system as self, non-self, or threat, and by what mechanisms a clone can escape that recognition while remaining biologically active.
The monograph proposes a two-axis antigenic map (when an antigen appeared relative to immune tolerance; where it is physically located in the cell), a catalog of basic immune-reaction mechanisms independent of context, and then applies both to six concrete cases — cancer, pregnancy, autoimmunity, transplantation, chronic infection, and regeneration — testing the same architecture against each. The central open hypothesis of the volume is the “double lock” model of dormancy: that a dormant clone must simultaneously satisfy an energetic lock (IGF1R–mTOR–NR2F1, from earlier volumes) and an immune lock (T/NK, phagocytic, and metabolic invisibility, developed here). Three competing architectures for how these two locks relate (Variants A, B, C) are left explicitly unresolved, pending direct experimental test.
